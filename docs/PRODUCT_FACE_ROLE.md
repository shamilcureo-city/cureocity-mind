> **Status:** draft for discussion (you + her). Nothing here is fixed —
> the open questions in § 10 are the agenda for your first conversation.
> **Owner of this doc:** founder. **Subject:** the psychologist who will be
> the face of Cureocity Mind and its field product manager.
>
> This is deliberately separate from `docs/PILOT_PLAYBOOK.md` (the

## 0. The one-sentence version

She is a psychologist who talks to psychologists — turning peer trust into
trials, and every trial conversation into product direction. Two hats,
one person: **the face out front, the product brain out back.**

Why this works: therapists don't buy from salespeople, they trust
colleagues. A credible clinician who _uses the tool and is honest about it_
is worth more than any ad. That credibility is the entire strategy — which
means protecting it is her first job (see § 8, guardrails).

## 1. The role

**Title (pick one — see § 10):**

- **Founding Clinical Lead** _(recommended — signals seniority + clinical
  credibility + "founding" ownership)_
- Clinical Product & Community Lead
- Head of Clinician Growth

**Mission:** get Cureocity Mind into the hands of the right therapists, make
the first ones successful, and be the voice that carries what they need back
into the product.

**The two hats, and why one person holds both:**

| Hat                       | Front / back | What it means                                                                                                      |
| ------------------------- | ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Product Face**          | Front        | The human therapists meet: coffees, demos, LinkedIn, the trust. When a therapist thinks "Cureocity," they see her. |
| **Field Product Manager** | Back         | The only person who hears _every_ user. She converts those conversations into ranked product signal for the build. |

These reinforce each other: because she's in every room, she's the best-
informed PM in the company; because she shapes the product, she can promise
users "I'll get that fixed" and mean it. That loop is the job.

**What she owns**

- The top of the funnel: getting therapists to a first conversation
  (coffee, video, LinkedIn).
- The conversation → trial conversion, and the trial → active-user
  activation (with founder on technical onboarding at first).
- The brand voice in the market: how Cureocity sounds to a clinician.
- The feedback flywheel: capturing, ranking, and championing what users
  need (§ 6).

**What she does _not_ own** (keep the boundary clean)

- Pricing, billing, legal, infra decisions — founder's call. She surfaces
  the signal ("three people balked at ₹X"); founder decides.
- Engineering priority _authority_ — she has the loudest voice for the
  user, but the founder owns the final roadmap call. Healthy tension by
  design.
- She is **not** a call-center chasing volume. Quality of conversation and
  trust beats quantity every time (see the realism note in § 2).

## 2. The GTM motion — the "coffee engine"

Your plan: **invite ~5 psychologists a day for coffee, she explains the
product and starts their trial.** Good instinct — in-person peer selling is
the highest-trust channel we have. Two refinements to make it survivable and
compounding:

**Reframe "pitch" → "conversation."** She is not pitching. She is a
clinician asking another clinician about their week — the notes, the
evenings lost to admin, the burnout — and _then_ showing a tool that helps.
Discovery first, demo second. People buy what they've told you they need.

**A realism note on "5 a day."** Five _in-person_ coffees a day is ~7–8
hours of meetings plus travel — it burns out in a week and leaves no time
for follow-up or content. Healthier target: **up to 5 real conversations a
day, blended** — say 2 in-person coffees + 2 video calls + 1 warm LinkedIn
thread — with in-person reserved for the highest-intent leads. That's
~20–25 quality conversations a week, which is plenty to fill a pilot. (Put
the exact number in § 10 and revisit after week 1 with real data.)

### 2a. Getting the coffee (the invite)

Channels, warmest first:

1. **Her own network** — training cohorts, supervision groups, ex-
   colleagues, WhatsApp clinical circles. Warm intros convert 5–10× cold.
2. **Referrals** — every good conversation ends with "who else should I be
   talking to?" (template already in `docs/pilot/outreach.md` § 2).
3. **LinkedIn** — see § 3.
4. **Clinics & training institutes** — one yes from a clinic owner can be
   3–5 therapists.

Use the existing message templates in **`docs/pilot/outreach.md`** — don't
rewrite them, they already have the warm-touch, referral, email, and
follow-up versions plus an objection cheat-sheet.

### 2b. The coffee itself (30–45 min structure)

| Minute | Phase                | What she does                                                                                                    |
| ------ | -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 0–5    | **Rapport**          | Peer to peer. Their practice, their caseload, their city. No product yet.                                        |
| 5–15   | **Their pain**       | "Walk me through your week. When do notes actually get written?" Let them say the evenings/admin/burnout thing.  |
| 15–30  | **Show, don't tell** | Live demo on **her own account** (or a seeded demo client) — record → note → clinical brief → share. Real, fast. |
| 30–40  | **The trial offer**  | "Want to try it on your own next session?" Set up the invite + first-session commitment.                         |
| 40–45  | **Consent + next**   | Walk the consent script; book the week-0 onboarding (§ 2c); ask for a referral.                                  |

**Demo honesty (non-negotiable):** if she's showing mock/demo output, she
says so. Never present AI-generated demo content as a real client's note.
This is the trust line — one faked demo to a clinician and the credibility
is gone.

### 2c. The trial handoff

We are **invite-only pilot**, not open signup — she's granting access, not
just a link. The setup is already scripted:

- Onboarding + passkey + first-3-clients + the supervised dry-run all live
  in **`docs/pilot/week0-runbook.md`** (the 45-min run-of-show).
- At first, **founder runs the technical week-0 sitting**; she runs the
  relationship. As she ramps (§ 7), she takes over onboarding too.
- The trial has real caps (trial session limit + cost circuit) — she sets
  the expectation that this is a genuine pilot with a success bar, not a
  free-forever giveaway.

### 2d. Follow-up cadence (where deals are actually won)

Most "no"s are just "not yet." One light-touch sequence:

- **+48h:** "Great meeting you — here's your access. Want me to sit with
  you for your first recording?"
- **Day 3–4:** one nudge only (template in outreach.md § 4).
- **After first real session:** "How did the note come out?" — this is the
  activation moment; catch problems same-day.
- **Day 14:** check the habit is forming (are they signing notes without
  her?).

### 2e. Tracking

Extend the **cohort tracker in `docs/pilot/outreach.md`** into a simple
daily sheet she owns:

`Date · Name · City · Source · Stage (invited→met→trial→active→paying) ·
Next action · Notes`

Five stages, one row per lead. This _is_ her CRM until volume justifies a
real one (§ 10).

## 3. LinkedIn — the always-on top of funnel

Coffees don't scale alone; LinkedIn keeps the funnel full between them. Two
motions:

**A. Outbound (1:1).** Peer DMs to therapists in target cities. Warm, short,
curious — never a copy-paste pitch. Pattern:

> _"Hi [name] — fellow [psych/therapist], I've been building something for
> therapists around the notes-and-admin load and I'm talking to a few
> clinicians in [city] about it. Not selling anything — genuinely want your
> take. Open to a coffee/call?"_

Do: reference something specific from their profile. Don't: send the same
line to 50 people (LinkedIn flags it and it reads as spam).

**B. Content (1:many).** She builds a personal brand as _the therapist who
thinks hard about clinical admin, measurement-based care, and tech done
responsibly._ Inbound follows.

- **Cadence:** 2–3 posts/week. Consistency > volume.
- **Voice:** peer, evidence-first, India-real, never hypey. (Same voice as
  the product — see § 5.)
- **Content pillars + starter ideas:**

| Pillar                           | Example posts                                                                                           |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| The admin-burden truth           | "How many hours a week do you actually lose to notes?"; the evenings-back story                         |
| Measurement-based care education | Why PHQ-9/GAD-7 tracking changes outcomes; what "reliable change" means; de-stigmatising measurement    |
| India-practice reality           | Code-mix sessions (Manglish/Hinglish) and why most tools choke on them; DPDP and client data in India   |
| Responsible clinical tech        | What AI should and shouldn't do in therapy; the human-confirms-everything principle; consent done right |
| Building in the open             | Behind-the-scenes of building for therapists; what pilot therapists taught us this week (no PHI, ever)  |

- **Profile positioning:** headline names both identities ("Psychologist ·
  building Cureocity Mind for Indian therapists"); About tells the _why_;
  Featured pins the best post + a way to reach her.

**Content guardrails:** never a real client's data or story (even
anonymised, err on the side of no); never overclaim AI accuracy; always
disclose she's building the product. A founder/she content-review pass in
the first month, then she runs solo (§ 10).

## 4. Being the face — brand, voice, trust

She _is_ the brand to every clinician she touches. Three things to hold:

**The voice.** Calm, peer, evidence-first, India-real, never salesy. Mirror
the product's own line — _"Get your evenings back. The co-pilot that writes
your notes and tracks your clients' progress while you focus on the work."_
Not "revolutionary AI." Just: this gives you your time back, honestly.

**The trust posture.** She's a psychologist selling to psychologists, so her
credibility is the asset — and it's fragile. The product's own honesty rules
are _her_ rules:

- Demo/mock content is always labelled as such.
- Never overpromise the AI — it drafts, the therapist confirms; it can be
  wrong, and she says so plainly.
- Client safety and privacy are sacred: consent before recording, DPDP/in-
  region processing, encrypted + audited. She can explain all of this in
  plain language (it's a selling point, not fine print).

**What she represents.** In a room, on a call, in a post: a thoughtful
clinician who found something genuinely useful and is sharing it with peers —
not a vendor. If a therapist ever feels _sold to_ instead of _understood_,
she's off-voice.

## 5. The Product Manager half — the feedback flywheel

She is the best-positioned PM we have, because she hears every user. Her PM
job is to convert conversations into **ranked product signal**.

**The weekly loop:**

1. **Capture** every friction point, confusion, and feature ask in a running
   feedback log (one line each: user, quote, what they were trying to do).
2. **Tag** each: `fix-now / fix-next / won't-fix`, and note frequency.
3. **Rank** by frequency × pain — the top 3 are what she champions.
4. **Bring them to the founder's Friday ritual** (`PILOT_PLAYBOOK.md` § 5) —
   she can co-run it. She represents the user; founder owns the call.
5. **Close the loop back to users:** "you asked, we shipped." This is
   retention gold and makes therapists feel like co-builders.

**What she's guarding:** the user's voice inside the roadmap. When
engineering wants to build X and users keep asking for Y, she's the one who
says so — with data (the tagged log), not vibes.

## 7. What success looks like for her

Two layers — the ones she controls (leading) and the ones that prove it
worked (lagging). The lagging ones are the **shared pilot criteria** from
`PILOT_PLAYBOOK.md` § 2, so there's one definition of success, not two.

**Leading (she owns these):**

- Conversations held per week (target set in § 10)
- Conversation → trial conversion rate
- Trial → active (recorded a real session) rate
- LinkedIn: posts shipped, inbound conversations generated

**Lagging (shared with the pilot):**

- ≥4 of 5 pilots still active in week 4
- ≥1 therapist says, unprompted, they'd pay
- Baseline + re-measure coverage (measurement-based care actually happening)

She's succeeding when the funnel fills itself (inbound from content +
referrals) and pilots stay active without hand-holding.

## 8. First 30 / 60 / 90 days (the ramp)

- **Days 0–30 — Learn + shadow.** Know the product cold (do the full
  record→note→sign→share loop herself, many times). Shadow the founder on
  2–3 week-0 onboardings. Set up her LinkedIn positioning. Start warm-
  network coffees (founder still runs technical onboarding).
- **Days 30–60 — Run solo.** Own the coffee funnel end to end. Start posting
  (2–3/week). Take over week-0 onboarding. Begin the weekly feedback log +
  join the Friday ritual.
- **Days 60–90 — Own the funnel + shape the product.** Inbound starting from
  content; referral loop turning. She's a real voice in roadmap calls with a
  ranked feedback log behind her. Decide the first trial→paying conversions.

---

## 9. Guardrails & non-negotiables

- **Client privacy / DPDP:** never handle or repeat real client data; consent
  before any recording; in-region processing and encryption are promises we
  keep, not marketing.
- **Clinical honesty:** the AI drafts, the human confirms. Never sell it as
  autonomous or infallible. Crisis/safety features exist to help the
  clinician, never to replace judgment.
- **Demo honesty:** mock content is always labelled; never shown as a real
  note.
- **Invite discipline:** it's a gated pilot — access is granted
  deliberately, not sprayed. Respect the trial caps and the success bar.
- **Safety incidents are stop-the-line:** a missed crisis flag or wrong-
  client share is escalated to the founder _immediately_, not at the Friday
  ritual (`PILOT_PLAYBOOK.md` § 7).

## 10. Open questions — the agenda for your first conversation

Decide these together; they're the real content of "defining her role."

1. **Title** — Founding Clinical Lead, or one of the alternatives in § 1?
2. **Comp + incentive** — base? equity (she's effectively a founding hire)?
   a bonus tied to active pilots or "would-pay" signals (careful: don't
   incentivise pushy selling that burns trust)?
3. **The real daily target** — is 5 the right number, and blended how?
   Set a starting number, commit to revisiting after week 1 with data.
4. **Geography** — one city deep (e.g. Bangalore) or spread? Deep usually
   wins early (referrals compound locally).
5. **Onboarding split** — how long does the founder stay on technical week-0
   before she owns it fully?
6. **Content approval** — founder reviews her posts for month 1, then she
   runs solo? Or trust from day 1?
7. **Tooling** — is a spreadsheet enough to start, or do we want a light CRM
   (HubSpot free / Notion) from the outset?
8. **Decision authority** — what can she decide alone (who to invite,
   messaging, content) vs. what always comes to the founder (pricing,
   promises about the roadmap, comp for referrals)?
9. **Her own success definition** — does she agree the § 7 scorecard is fair
   and mostly within her control?
